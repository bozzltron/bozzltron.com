This series is about influential music and what it did to me. It’s inspired by Jeff Tweedy’s book *World Within a Song: Music That Changed My Life and Life That Changed My Music*. After *Tomorrow*, I’m turning to *Black Hole Sun*.

<!-- more -->

*Black Hole Sun* is the song a lot of people mean when they say “Soundgarden.” Chris Cornell wrote it alone. It came out as the **third single** from *Superunknown* in **May 1994**. The LP itself had landed in **March**, so MTV and FM caught it while *Superunknown* was still fresh—not a deep‑catalog sleeper but a centerpiece from one of Seattle’s definitive major‑label eras. Radio and MTV inflated it into **one of the defining summer rock tracks of 1994**: it logged **seven weeks at number one** on Billboard’s **Album Rock Tracks** chart (today’s mainstream rock lineage) and ranked at the **top of year‑end alternative airplay** lists as well.

The actual birth of the song was almost mundane. Cornell told journalists—most famously in an **August 2014 Uncut** piece later republished online—that he **composed it in his head on the drive home** from **Bear Creek Studio** in **Woodinville** (roughly 35–40 minutes northeast of Seattle). A TV anchor said something Cornell **misheard** as “blah blah blah **black hole sun** blah blah blah.” The phrase stuck as a title question: *what would a song called that sound like?*

He stayed with it long enough that he claims the bulk of it arrived together—**melody, changes, even the notion of the guitar solo sitting under the arpeggiated riff**. To keep from losing it, he **whistled what he’d worked out into a Dictaphone** when he pulled in at home; the **next day** he started moving it onto an instrument (**he also mentioned sketching it on a Gretsch**) and tweaked the verses with extra key shifts so they wouldn’t bore him. Lyrics came afterward, **mostly stream‑of‑consciousness**—“painting,” as he sometimes put it, off the chorus feeling rather than a literal topic sentence. He guessed the band might balk—it was prettier and more psychedelic‑pop than Soundgarden’s riff‑forward reputation—and recalled writing very fast (**about fifteen minutes**, in some accounts).

The finished album track was tracked at **Bad Animals** in **Seattle** with **producer Michael Beinhorn** credited alongside the band (**Brendan O’Brien** engineered). Cornell leaned on **Leslie‑cabinet swirl**—Kim Thayil later described its **Beatles‑y** halo—and in anniversary interviews Cornell confessed nerd‑worthy details fans love: chasing the demo’s elusive “sparkle” involved not only hauling the odd **Fender/Leslie combo** rig back into the studio but realizing he’d **vari‑sped his demo playback** slightly faster before singing against it (**Beinhorn** once described hearing the band play through the tune for him as unforgettable—like getting hit by a thunderbolt—from the booth). Cornell also talked about punching in **many vocal takes** across **comped lines** until the performance justified the hypnotic refrain everyone already couldn’t shake.

Promotion followed reality: **Howard Greenhalgh** directed the famously unsettling suburb‑meltdown promo; after **120 Minutes** on **June 12, 1994**, MTV couldn’t peel it down. Cornell said the clip was intentionally **hands‑off creatively**—“pretend we’re bored on a green screen”—so the director could lean into juxtaposition rather than choreography. Critics split on whether it’s “mostly mood” versus “canonical pop songwriting,” yet even skeptics conceded the Lennon‑ish melody and dripping guitar hypnosis.

**Where the music landed for me.** *(Draft — tighten in your voice.)* Cornell called the lyrics **surreal word‑play**, not sermon; critics heard **psychedelia colliding with grunge’s weight**. I still need to articulate what that contrast did inside my headphones—is it the creeping dread under a singalong? Thayil’s solo as weather system?—

**What it finally gave me—as a musician, as a person.** *(Draft — your story.)* Permission? Permission to go strange and still melodic? Compass for odd tunings off *Superunknown*? Meeting Seattle’s melancholy glamour at fourteen?—
